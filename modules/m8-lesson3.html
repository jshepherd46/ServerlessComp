The whole picture

Two tiers, one architecture

The single most important architectural commitment SRL makes is the production-PHI / research-de-id split. Production PHI lives in a tightly contained tier; every research workload reads from a sibling de-id'd tier. Data moves once, from production into research, through controlled de-identification. Researchers never touch production PHI; production never accumulates research workloads.

Why the two-tier shape wins

🔒
PHI never moves laterally
Researchers, training pipelines, exploratory notebooks all read the de-id tier. PHI lives in one place and stays there.
📊
Audit posture is clean
Every read of PHI happens in the production tier and is Data Access logged (M3L3). Research-tier reads are still logged but the data is no longer PHI.
⚖️
Scale-independent
Heavy ML training in the research tier never competes with production ingest for resources. Different projects, different quotas, different IAM.
🧬
Reproducibility
De-id tier snapshots are versioned (date-stamped exports + Vertex Pipeline runs). A paper from 2024 can be re-derived from the 2024 snapshot, not the 2026 live state.
The discipline this requires. Every new study answers two questions on day one: which production-tier resources does this study consume? and which de-id'd resources does this study produce / use? The shape is decided up-front, in the M1L4 runbook, before any data flows.
From request to dataset

The cohort instantiation pattern

A researcher emails: "For our manuscript I need DICOM studies of women 40-65 with prior screening mammograms in 2020-2024 with biopsy-confirmed outcomes." Eight steps from request to working cohort.

Step 1 — IRB
Researcher files an IRB protocol describing the cohort, the data fields needed, the analysis plan, and (critically) what counts as a successful end-of-study data retirement. Without an approved protocol, no further steps fire.
Step 2 — Cohort spec
A formal cohort definition - SQL against the production-tier BigQuery (PHI), or an ATLAS cohort (M9) against OMOP. The query is reviewed by the data steward and stored in a versioned cohort-specs repository. The query is run against PHI but its output is just patient IDs + study UIDs, not data.
Step 3 — De-id'd corpus build
A Vertex Pipeline (M6L2 pattern) reads the cohort patient list, fetches the corresponding records from the production tier under a tightly-scoped audit-logged SA, runs DLP + Healthcare API de-id (M8L2), writes a per-cohort GCS / BigQuery dataset into the research tier. The cohort\'s patient surrogates are derived from the global surrogate map, so linkage to other cohorts works.
Step 4 — Grant access
The de-id\'d cohort dataset gets bound to a per-cohort group (e.g. halo-cohort-2026-mammo@). Researchers added to the group; IAM Conditions limit access by date (matching the IRB-approved timeline). Per M1L3 — groups, not individuals.
Step 5 — Researcher consumes
Researcher queries BigQuery, trains models, exports figures. All inside the research tier; Data Access logging captures who read what within the cohort dataset. Outputs (figures, model artifacts) stay in research tier projects.
Step 6 — Publish
Figures and aggregate results from the cohort can leave for the manuscript. Per HIPAA Safe Harbor + IRB rules, what specifically leaves is reviewed by the steward (and the IRB if specified). Individual-level data never leaves.
Step 7 — Archive
At paper acceptance, the cohort dataset snapshots into a research-archive bucket (CMEK, immutable, in shared-services). The Vertex Pipeline run that produced it is preserved with its parameters. Re-deriving the figure five years later from the archive snapshot is one pipeline rerun.
Step 8 — Retire
Per the IRB-defined end-of-study, the cohort dataset is deleted from active research tier; the access group is dissolved; access logs are retained per institutional policy. The archive snapshot persists; current access goes to zero.
What this discipline gives you. Every research-tier dataset answers: "What\'s the IRB protocol? Who\'s allowed in? What surrogates? Where\'s the spec? When does it retire?" From M1L4\'s runbook through to the archive bucket, every cohort has a single canonical record. The next grant audit, the next paper revision, the next student joining the lab — all are easier when the answer to each question is documented.
Knowing it works

Operational metrics for the data pipeline

M4L3 set up SLOs for serving services. The same discipline applies to the data pipeline — different metrics, same shape. Four worth tracking from the day the pipeline goes live.

1. Ingest freshness

The lag from "an EHR or PACS produced data at time T" to "that data is in the production-tier store." Goal: usually <1 hour for streaming ingest, <24 hours for nightly batch. Alert when freshness exceeds the goal — a stuck ingest means the next 24 hours of research operates on stale data.

FRESHNESS = NOW - MAX(resource.last_updated). Run as a scheduled BigQuery query against the FHIR store\'s BQ streaming tier; emit to Cloud Monitoring.

2. De-id completeness

The fraction of production-tier records that have been propagated through de-id to the research tier. A drop here means the de-id pipeline is failing silently — records are flowing in but not getting de-id\'d for research use. Alert when completeness drops below a threshold (95% within 24 hours of ingest).

COMPLETENESS = COUNT(deid_resources) / COUNT(production_resources) over a recent window.

3. De-id quality (sample-based)

A scheduled inspect pass against a random sample of de-id\'d records, looking for missed PHI. Should be near zero. Any non-zero finding triggers a manual review and possibly a de-id template revision (M8L2).

Run DLP inspect on a 0.1% sample weekly; emit findings count to Cloud Monitoring; alert on any finding above LIKELY threshold.

4. Cohort-build SLO

From "researcher submits cohort spec" to "researcher has access to the de-id\'d cohort dataset": typically 24-72 hours, including the data-steward review. Track per-cohort; surface the median + 90th percentile monthly. The trend matters more than any single value — increasing build time signals the pipeline is under-resourced.

The dashboard

Each metric in Cloud Monitoring as a custom metric; one dashboard per pipeline (HIPIMR ingest, ShepBERT corpus, HALO OMOP). The metric you check on Monday morning answers "is the pipeline still working?" without you reading individual logs.

The discipline tradeoff. Each metric costs to set up — 1-2 hours of engineering. Skipping them costs ~one incident before the lab regrets it. Build them when the pipeline goes live, not after the first time you scramble to answer "did anything go wrong last week?"
Hands-on exercise 8.3

Map your own study's data pipeline end to end

📐 Architecture-on-paper, then implementation gaps
  1. Pick a real study you run (or want to run). HIPIMR, Makawalu, HALO, ShepBERT, MAPS — whichever has the freshest pipeline in your head.
  2. Map the production tier: source system (EHR/PACS), ingest mechanism, Healthcare API store(s) used, streaming destinations (BigQuery), CMEK keys, perimeter membership.
  3. Map the research tier: de-id\'d stores, what training corpora they materialize into, who reads them (which groups, which workload SAs), the Vertex Pipeline(s) that produce them.
  4. Map the data-steward review path: who reviews cohort specs, who approves access, who runs the de-id pipeline, who archives at end of study. If any of these are "the same person" or "nobody," that\'s a gap.
  5. List the operational metrics you currently track vs the four from Section 3. Note the gaps.
  6. Pick two metrics from the gap list and design how you\'d implement them: query, scheduling, alerting policy, who gets paged. This is the M1L4 runbook\'s data-pipeline section.
  7. Optional — implement one. Pick the cheapest metric (probably ingest freshness via a scheduled BQ query); set up the Cloud Monitoring custom metric + dashboard tile; verify it produces a number.
  8. Document the cohort-instantiation workflow for your study: who reviews requests, what an accepted cohort spec looks like, where the de-id pipeline runs, how access is granted. Save as part of the lab\'s runbook (M1L4).
  9. One sanity check. For the next cohort that lands on your desk, walk through the eight steps of Section 2 explicitly. Note any step where your process doesn\'t match the template — that\'s the next improvement.
